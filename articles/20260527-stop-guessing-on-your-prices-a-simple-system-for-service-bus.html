Stop Guessing on Your Prices: A Simple System for Service Businesses

How I went from undercharging and overworking to finally knowing exactly what to charge — and why it changed everything.

For the first three years of running my own consulting business, I priced my services the same way most service business owners do: I panicked, made something up, and hoped the client said yes.

I'd look at what a competitor was charging — or what I thought they were charging, because nobody actually tells you — and I'd either match it or go slightly lower because I was terrified of losing the work. Then I'd spend the entire project feeling resentful, overextended, and quietly convinced I was running a very elaborate charity.

Sound familiar? If you've ever finished a client project and thought, I worked way too hard for this money, then this article is for you.

The Project That Finally Broke Me

About four years ago, I landed what felt like a dream client. A mid-sized company, friendly people, a clear scope of work. I quoted them $3,800 for what I estimated would be about three weeks of focused work. They said yes immediately — and I should have taken that as a warning sign, because when a client agrees without flinching, it usually means you left money on the table.

The project ran six weeks. The scope crept in every direction. I ended up billing roughly $11 an hour when I did the math afterward. My phone bill costs more than that per hour of actual value.

But here's the thing — I couldn't even be mad at the client. They'd agreed to exactly what I'd offered. The problem was mine. I had no system. I had no method. I was guessing, and it was costing me thousands of dollars and a significant piece of my sanity.

That project was the turning point. I decided I was going to figure out pricing once and for all, not just set a number and hope, but actually build a real framework I could apply to every engagement going forward.

Why Service Business Pricing Is So Hard

Here's what makes pricing services different from pricing a physical product. If you sell a chair, there's a cost of goods. Materials, labor, shipping — you can build up from there. It's math. Uncomfortable math sometimes, but math.

With services, you're selling time, expertise, judgment, and outcomes. None of those have a sticker price. There's no supply chain. There's no invoice from a manufacturer you can point to. It's just you, your brain, and the value you create — which is genuinely hard to quantify, especially when you're too close to your own work to see it clearly.

Most service providers default to one of three broken approaches. They charge hourly because it feels fair and transparent, without realizing that it punishes efficiency and gives clients a reason to question every line item. They copy a competitor's price without knowing anything about that competitor's costs, positioning, or target client. Or they reverse-engineer from what they think a client will pay, which is just anxiety dressed up as strategy.

None of these work long-term. They all leave value uncaptured and resentment slowly building up underneath the surface of every client relationship.

The Framework That Actually Works

What finally helped me was stepping back and building pricing from three directions simultaneously — and then triangulating between them to find a number I could defend and feel good about.

The first direction is costs, and I mean real costs. Not just your hourly rate times some number of hours. Every subscription you use for that project. Every tool, platform, or subcontractor. A portion of your rent or home office, your internet, your insurance. Your own compensation — the money you need to actually live your life, pay taxes, contribute to savings, and not feel like you're running yourself into the ground. When you add all of this up and divide it by the number of billable hours you can realistically sell in a month, you get your floor. The absolute minimum you can charge without literally losing money. Most people have never done this calculation. Most people are shocked by it.

The second direction is market positioning. What do the strongest players in your niche charge, and more importantly, why? Price is a signal. If you undercharge dramatically relative to your market, it raises questions about your quality before you've even had a first conversation. This doesn't mean you need to be the most expensive option, but you do need to understand where you sit in the landscape and make that choice consciously rather than by default.

The third direction — and this is the one most people skip entirely — is value delivered. What is this project actually worth to the client? If you're a copywriter and your sales page generates $400,000 in revenue for a client, charging $1,500 for it is a catastrophic underpricing, regardless of how many hours it took you. If you're a bookkeeper who helps a business owner stop overpaying in taxes by $18,000 a year, your monthly retainer should reflect some portion of that value, not just your time. Getting comfortable with value-based pricing requires a mindset shift that doesn't happen overnight, but it is the single most powerful change you can make to your revenue.

If you want to go deep on building that mindset, Pricing Creativity by Blair Enns (affiliate link) is one of the best frameworks I've found for service businesses specifically. It challenged a lot of the assumptions I'd been carrying around for years about what it means to charge for expertise.

Putting It Into Practice: What to Do This Week

The first thing I'd encourage you to do is run your actual cost calculation. Pull up your bank statements from the last three months. Categorize everything related to running your business. Add a line for your own salary — what you actually need to pay yourself, not what you've been paying yourself. Divide by the number of hours you realistically bill per month, which for most solo operators is somewhere between 80 and 120. That number is your floor. Write it down. You may never charge exactly that number, but you should never charge below it.

Next, spend an hour researching what others in your niche charge. Look at their websites. Look at their proposals if any are publicly available. Talk to peers if you have relationships in your industry. Join a professional community or forum where pricing gets discussed openly. You don't need a perfect picture — you just need enough information to place yourself on the map deliberately.

Then start having better discovery conversations with clients. Before you ever quote a price, get into the habit of asking what this project means to them in real business terms. What happens if this problem isn't solved? What would a successful outcome actually do for their revenue, their time, their sanity? The answers to those questions will tell you more about what to charge than any competitor research ever will.

One resource I return to regularly for the business-finance side of all of this is Profit First by Mike Michalowicz (affiliate link). It's not specifically about pricing, but it completely rewired how I think about money flowing through my business, and once I got my financial structure right, pricing decisions became so much clearer.

Finally, practice raising your prices. I know that sounds obvious, but it doesn't feel obvious when you're sitting across from a client — or sending a proposal — and your palms are sweating. The only way to get comfortable with higher prices is to quote them, watch clients say yes, and slowly rebuild your confidence that the market will bear what you're worth. Start with new clients. You don't have to renegotiate everything overnight. But the next proposal you send, push the number up by fifteen or twenty percent and see what happens. You might be surprised.

The Quiet Shift That Changes Everything

Here's what I've noticed since building an actual system around my pricing: the conversations I have with clients are different. I'm not apologetic. I'm not bracing for rejection. I present my prices with the same energy I present my work — like I believe in it, because I do, because I've done the math and I know what it's worth.

And clients pick up on that. Confidence is contagious. When you flinch at your own number, clients flinch too. When you say it calmly and move on, they tend to do the same.

There's also a longer-term effect I didn't anticipate. When you're priced correctly, you have more capacity. You're not cramming in extra clients to make ends meet. You're not burning through weekends on underpaid work. You have time to do better work, to think more clearly, to actually serve your clients at the level they deserve. Good pricing is good for the clients too, even if they don't know it.

I'm not going to pretend this is easy or that I got it right immediately. I still second-guess myself sometimes. I still occasionally finish a project and wonder if I left something on the table. But I'm no longer in the dark. I have a framework, I have a floor, and I have the confidence to defend what I charge — and that has made all the difference.
