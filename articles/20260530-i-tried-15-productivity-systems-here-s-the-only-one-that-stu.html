There's a specific kind of shame that comes with having seventeen half-filled planners in your desk drawer. I know because I've counted mine.

I'm the person who spent a full Sunday afternoon color-coding a new task management system, felt an enormous wave of satisfaction, and then abandoned it completely by Wednesday. I've done this more times than I care to admit. And somewhere along the way, I convinced myself that the problem was always the system — that I just hadn't found the right one yet.

Spoiler: that wasn't the problem.

Let me back up. I started taking productivity seriously around 2019, when I left a corporate marketing job to run my own consulting business. Suddenly, nobody was setting my schedule. Nobody was telling me which fires to put out first. I had clients, deadlines, invoices, content to create, and a brain that kept insisting we should reorganize the kitchen instead of doing literally any of it.

So I did what any sensible, slightly anxious entrepreneur does: I started researching. I read blog posts, watched YouTube videos, listened to podcasts on 1.5x speed while pretending that counted as being productive. I tried GTD — Getting Things Done — David Allen's legendary system, with its inboxes and contexts and weekly reviews. It lasted about three weeks before the weekly review turned into a monthly review turned into a "I'll get to that someday" review.

After GTD, I moved on. Time blocking. The Pomodoro Technique. The Eisenhower Matrix. Eat the Frog. The 1-3-5 rule. Personal Kanban boards. The Bullet Journal method, which I was absolutely convinced would be the one — I bought nice pens and everything. I went deep on Notion, building elaborate dashboards that were genuinely beautiful and almost completely useless for actually getting work done. I tried OmniFocus, Todoist, TickTick, Things 3, and a brief, regrettable affair with a whiteboard wall decal in my home office.

Each system gave me the same initial high. That feeling of control, of clarity, of this time it's different. And each one eventually collapsed under the weight of real life — a busy week, a sick kid, a client emergency — and I'd fall off and never get back on.

By early 2024, I was exhausted. Not from working too much, but from the constant overhead of managing how I was supposed to be working.

Here's the thing nobody tells you about productivity systems: most of them are designed by people whose entire job is to think about productivity. They have assistants. They have speaking fees. They have the luxury of treating their own workflow as a full-time optimization project. For the rest of us — the ones actually running businesses, raising families, trying to sleep enough — a system that requires thirty minutes of daily maintenance isn't a system. It's another job.

The deeper problem, which took me an embarrassingly long time to see, is that I was using productivity research as a form of productive procrastination. Every hour I spent reading about systems was an hour I didn't have to spend doing the uncomfortable, uncertain work of actually building something. The optimization felt like progress. It wasn't.

I also didn't have a clear answer to a pretty fundamental question: productive toward what, exactly? I had a vague sense of wanting to grow my business, make more money, and have time left over for my family. But I hadn't sat down and made those things concrete. So every new system I tried was essentially a powerful engine with no steering wheel.

The shift happened almost by accident. I'd been going through a rough patch — a major client pulled out of a contract, I was behind on a project, and I felt genuinely stuck. A friend recommended I read Deep Work by Cal Newport (affiliate link), which had been on my list forever and somehow kept getting bumped. I finally sat down with it on a Tuesday night and read half of it in one sitting.

Newport's argument isn't really about tactics. It's about philosophy. The idea that the ability to focus without distraction is becoming simultaneously rarer and more valuable — and that if you can cultivate it, you have a genuine competitive advantage. It reframed everything for me. I'd been asking "what system should I use?" when the real question was "what kind of worker do I want to be?"

I stopped trying to find a perfect system and started building one small habit: every morning, before I opened email, before I looked at Slack, before I did anything reactive at all, I would spend ninety minutes on the single most important thing I was working on. No phone. No tabs. Just that one thing.

That's it. That was the whole system at the start.

It sounds laughably simple, and I want to be honest — the first two weeks were uncomfortable. I'd sit down and feel the pull of my inbox like a physical sensation. I'd catch myself opening a browser tab just to check something "quick." But slowly, slowly, it started to work. I finished a proposal that had been sitting half-done for two weeks. I wrote a content series I'd been "planning" for months. I started shipping things instead of just thinking about shipping things.

Over time, I layered in a few other pieces. On Sunday evenings, I spend about fifteen minutes — not an hour, fifteen minutes — writing down the three things that would make the following week feel successful. Not a full life audit. Just three things. On Fridays, I take ten minutes to write down what I actually accomplished and what got in the way. That's my entire weekly review. It fits on a single page of a simple notebook I keep on my desk.

I also got much more intentional about my physical workspace. This might sound trivial, but it isn't. I'd been working in a messy corner of my living room for two years, constantly interrupted, constantly distracted. I cleared out a spare room, set it up properly, and started using a dedicated daily planner (affiliate link) instead of trying to run everything through an app. There's something about writing things down by hand that makes them feel real and finalized in a way that typing doesn't. The planner became my anchor — each morning I'd write the date, my one big focus for the day, and a short list of smaller tasks. Nothing else.

The digital tools didn't disappear entirely. I still use a task manager for capturing things I don't want to forget, and my calendar is still the final word on where my time goes. But these became supports for the system rather than the system itself. The system is the intention I set before I touch any of them.

What I've learned, after five-plus years of trial and error and seventeen half-filled planners, comes down to a few things I wish someone had told me earlier.

The best productivity system is the one you'll actually use on your worst days, not your best ones. When everything's going smoothly, any system looks like it works. The real test is Tuesday afternoon when you're tired, something went sideways, and you have three hours left. What do you actually do? Build your habits for that version of yourself, not the inspired Sunday-morning version.

Your system has to be connected to outcomes you genuinely care about. Before you adopt any new tool or method, spend twenty minutes writing down what "a great year" looks like for you — specifically, concretely. Your system exists to serve those outcomes. If it doesn't, it's just theater.

Simpler is almost always better. The most elaborate system I built — the Notion dashboard with linked databases and automated task rollups — required so much maintenance that maintaining it became the task. My current system can be explained in three sentences. That's not laziness. That's hard-won wisdom.

And finally, the point is never the system. The point is the work. The conversations with clients, the creative output, the time you spend fully present with the people you love. The system is supposed to protect that time and that energy — not consume it.

I still have those seventeen planners in the drawer. I haven't thrown them out because honestly they're a useful reminder. Every time I feel the itch to overhaul everything and start fresh with some shiny new method, I open that drawer and remember how that story ends.

Instead, I close the drawer, make a cup of coffee, sit down, and do the work.

If you've been on the same merry-go-round — chasing systems, buying apps, reading books and never quite changing anything — I hope something here lands for you. Not because my system is the right one for everyone, but because the act of simplifying and actually committing to something, anything, is how you finally get off the ride.

Start with ninety minutes tomorrow morning. Before email. Before the news. Before anything that pulls your attention outward. Spend it on the thing that actually matters. See what happens.
