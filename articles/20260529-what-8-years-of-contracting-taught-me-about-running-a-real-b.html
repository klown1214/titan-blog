I remember the exact moment I realized I had no idea what I was doing.

It was a Tuesday in October, somewhere around year three of running my own contracting business. I was sitting in my truck outside a client's house, staring at a spreadsheet on my laptop that looked like someone had sneezed numbers across the screen. I had three jobs running simultaneously, two guys who hadn't been paid on time because I'd miscalculated materials on a bathroom remodel, and a checking account that told me I'd made about $11,000 that month. But I couldn't explain where any of it went. The work was good. Clients were happy. And somehow I was still broke on a Tuesday in October, eating a gas station sandwich and questioning every decision I'd ever made.

That's the version of contracting nobody talks about when they're hyping up the trades on YouTube.

I got into this the way a lot of guys do. I'd been working for a general contractor since I was nineteen, learned framing, picked up finish carpentry on the side, and by my mid-twenties I was doing side jobs on weekends that were pulling in more than my day rate. The math seemed obvious. If I was making my boss money, why wasn't I the boss? So I got my license, bought a used van, printed some business cards at Staples, and told myself I was in business.

And in a way, I was. Just not the kind of business that was going to survive.

For the first two years, I worked constantly. Twelve, fourteen hour days. I was good at the craft — genuinely good — and word spread fast in my area. I never had a slow week. But I also never had a plan. I priced jobs based on gut feeling and what I thought clients could afford. I paid for materials out of my personal account. I kept "records" in a notebook that I'd lose and then recreate from memory. My accountant, God bless him, spent half of our first meeting just staring at me.

The business wasn't a business. It was me doing skilled labor and collecting cash with no real system holding it together.

Here's the problem that most contractors run into, and I'd bet serious money it's the same problem you're dealing with right now if you're reading this: we are trained to be exceptional at the work, and completely untrained to run a company. These are two entirely different skill sets, and nobody in the trades tells you that when you're coming up.

You can frame a wall perfectly and still go bankrupt. You can tile a shower that looks like it belongs in a magazine and still underbid every job you touch. Skill in the field does not automatically translate into business sense, and the longer you pretend it does, the more expensive that lesson gets.

For me, the wake-up call came in year four when I lost a $34,000 remodel job because my bid was so disorganized that the homeowner didn't trust me to manage their project. She told me to my face: "Your work looks great, but your proposal looks like you put it together in a parking lot." She wasn't wrong. I had. I lost that job to a guy who charged more than me and whose crew I knew was mediocre. But his paperwork looked professional, and mine looked like a ransom note.

That stung. And it changed how I thought about everything.

I spent the next several months obsessively trying to understand the business side of what I was doing. I talked to contractors who had been running operations for twenty or thirty years. I took an online course on construction accounting. I started reading everything I could get my hands on. One of the books that actually made a difference was Markup and Profit: A Contractor's Guide Revisited by Michael Stone (affiliate link) — it sounds dry but it completely rewired how I thought about pricing. Before that book, I was quoting jobs to win them. After it, I was quoting jobs to actually make money on them. That distinction sounds simple and it is absolutely not simple when you're in the middle of it.

The biggest thing I learned from that period wasn't a tactic or a formula. It was a mindset shift. I stopped thinking of myself as a carpenter who happened to have a business, and started thinking of myself as a business owner who happened to do carpentry. That sounds like a LinkedIn platitude but I mean it literally. When I made that shift, every decision looked different. How I hired people. How I talked to clients. How I structured my days. All of it changed.

So what actually works? Here's what I'd tell the version of me sitting in that truck eating that terrible sandwich.

First, you have to get honest about your numbers before anything else. Not your revenue — your actual numbers. What does it cost you to run your operation per month before you touch a single job? Truck payments, insurance, tools, software, the time you spend estimating and doing admin. Most contractors I've talked to have never actually calculated their real overhead, and so they have no idea what their break-even point is. You cannot price correctly without knowing this number. Sit down this week and figure it out. Write it on a sticky note and put it on your dashboard if you have to.

Second, stop pricing from your gut. I know it feels like experience, but gut pricing is just optimism with math attached. Get yourself a real estimating system. It doesn't have to be complicated — I started with a simple spreadsheet before I moved to anything fancier — but it needs to be consistent and it needs to account for everything. Labor, materials, overhead, and profit as a separate line item. Profit is not what's left over. Profit is something you plan for. If you're not planning for it, you're not getting it.

Third, your contracts need to protect you. I cannot count how many jobs I did in my first few years on a handshake or a one-page agreement I found online. Scope creep alone probably cost me $20,000 over those years. Get a real contract template, have an attorney look at it once, and use it on every single job no matter how much a client says "oh we trust you, we don't need all that." The clients who say that are often the same ones who call you six months later telling you the paint looks slightly different in afternoon light and they're not paying the final draw.

Fourth, learn to read a financial statement. I don't mean become an accountant. I mean spend a few hours understanding what a profit and loss statement is telling you, what your cash flow actually looks like month over month, and why those two things are not the same thing. A business can be profitable on paper and still run out of cash. That happens to contractors constantly because of how projects are structured — you're often spending money for weeks before you see payment. Understanding this dynamic saved me from some genuinely close calls in years five and six.

Fifth, hire slower than you think you should. I made the mistake of hiring to meet demand instead of hiring to build a team, and there's a real difference. When you hire reactively, you end up with whoever is available, and available isn't always good. I had a guy for eight months who was personable, showed up on time, and did consistently mediocre work that I had to redo or apologize for on three separate jobs. The cost of that wasn't just his wages — it was my reputation. Take the time to hire people whose work you've actually seen.

One other resource that genuinely helped me sharpen my estimating process was getting a solid reference on construction cost data — I use RSMeans Cost Data construction estimating reference (affiliate link) as a sanity check when I'm bidding anything outside my usual scope. It's the kind of book that sits on your desk and earns its price back on the first job where you would have otherwise guessed wrong on labor hours.

Eight years in, I run a company now. Not just a job. I have a small crew I trust, a process for estimating that I could walk someone else through, contracts that have held up twice when things went sideways, and a checking account I actually understand. I'm not some massive operation — I don't want to be — but I'm profitable, I sleep reasonably well, and I haven't eaten a gas station sandwich in a truck while questioning my life choices in at least two years.

The craft is what got me in the door. But learning to run the business is what kept me there.

If you're in the early years of this thing and it feels like you're constantly working hard without getting ahead, you're probably not bad at contracting. You're probably just running a trade without running a business. Those aren't the same thing, and the gap between them is where most good contractors quietly disappear.

You don't have to disappear.
